Robert Antoine Pinchon
1866 - 1943

 

 

 

 



 


 


 

 

Robert Antoine Pinchon was the second and last child of the Pinchon-Dehame family. He was born on July 1, 1866.

The father of the young boy was born in Rouen in 1846, brought up in a literary and artistic environment, and became a close friend of Guy de Maupassant. After a stay in Paris, he returned to Rouen in 1880, and became a librarian and also a journalist, first at the “Nouvelliste” and later at the “Journal de Rouen” as a theater critic. In 1894, he published a collection of “theater” with his principal plays: Jacques Gautier, Richard-Without-Fear, Suzanne Dumanoir, Fuchs, A Storm, and Hunter Love. The book is dedicated “to my friend Guy de Maupassant”, who died on July 6, 1893.

In 1897 in “Rouen Silhouettes”, Dr. E. Spalikowski gave a friendly insight of the father of the young boy: “... theater critic, Mr. Pinchon is above all a poet....but the main stumbling block for the author is the inaptitude of the actors, who mangle piteously the verses, or recite them in such a way that the audience soon falls asleep...”

Young Robert Antoine studied at the Corneille Lycée, and soon fell in love with drawing and paintings, and at the age of 12 he painted his first canvas. At the Lycée he met Pierre Dumont and Marcel Duchamp. In March 1900, he exhibited his first painting in the gallery of Messrs. Dejonghe and Dumont, rue de la Républicque in Rouen. G. Dubosc writes: “...At that moment you could see the early work of a very young Rouen painter...this landscape of the hills of St. Adrien is a study of land bristling with junipers, which already exhibit happy qualities of vision and a solid temperament.” He studied at the Ecole des Beaux-arts de Rouen, where Zacharie taught. In 1903, at the exhibition of the Society of Friends of the Arts of Rouen, the young artist, age 17, hung two paintings on the walls of the museum. In July, at the prize giving at school, he received a medal of excellence for drawing. In September of that same year, he exhibited in Dieppe.

In May 1904, the young Robert, thanks to his father’s connections, designed the cover of the program of the Normandy Theater, which showed the “Castle of Miromesnil with the turret where Guy de Maupassant was born, and the villas of Guilette in Étretat”.

Francois Depeaux, an informed collector and remarkable supporter of Joseph Delattre and Charles Frechon, took control of the career of the young artist by buying a large part of his production.

In December 1904, the Galerie Legrip opened an exhibition hall in Place Sr. Armand, and Charles Frechon had the honor of inaugurating it, followed by Delattre in February and then Pinchon.

Just 19, Robert Pinchon faced the Rouen public and in April 1905 exhibited a group of 24 paintings. This precocious manifestation was hailed by G. Dubosc and E. Morel who wrote: “...We sincerely congratulate M. R. Pinchon for the enormous effort which this exhibition represents, and the general quality is excellent. It reveals a very organized mind and once he is certain of his direction, will give us remarkable works which will honor this “Rouen School”, whose revelation M. Legrip facilitates so freely to our citizens, who up until now have been the only ones who didn’t know about it....”.

During the summer, Robert Pinchon again sent a painting to the Society of Friends of the Arts of Dieppe and participated in the third Autumn Salon from October 18 to November 25 in Paris, the year of the “Cage of the Wild Beasts”. The next year, Robert Pinchon exhibited two works at the 37th Municipal Salon of Rouen and prepared the decoration of the bar-covered gallery Alhambra, representing the rustic restaurant of Le Bouille, then exhibited a painting at the Autumn Salon in Paris.

At age 21, the young artist responded favorably to P. Mascart and M. Delaunay, who had just started the Society of Rouen Artists; Pinchon sent six canvases for this first salon. Having formed a close friendship with Pierre Dumont, he followed with great interest the creation of the Group of XXX. A. M. Gossez, a writer and member of the group, explained the necessity of its creation in a letter: “...The eclecticism of the Society of Rouen Artists is excessive, it has a series of exhibitors, who would not distort the masters of chromolithography: however, several of the XXX have made a place there - not without difficulty - they appeared extremely “fauve”...Our judgment is more towards Cezanne and Manet, than towards Monet, although some impressionists like Frechon are attached to you...”

The first exhibition of the Group of XXX took place at Galerie Legrip from October 29 to November 12, and among the 40 paintings of Braque, Derain and Vlaminck, Robert Pinchon exhibited the “Champ de Blé” (Wheat Field).

After this surprising exhibition, the Galerie Legrip hosted the Autumn Exhibition, organized by the Management Committee of the Rouen Artists, where Pinchon exhibited a landscape. He participated in the “Almanac for 1908”, edited by the Group of XXX, with 450 copies. From March 7 to April 15, he sent six canvases for the second Rouen Artists Salon and spent 1908 preparing a series of “Quais de Rouen” and “Rocks of St. Adrien”.

Francois Depeaux became more and more interested in the production of the young artist. On September 23, 1908 he wrote to Pinchon “...Concerning the canvases that you brought to Lescure, I find them much better than the previous ones, and I would be disposed to buy two at the price you indicated to me or 3 for 500F. I would also buy your “July 14” but after some small changes, which I must discuss with you in person. A word of reply please. Your devoted Depeaux”.

At the beginning of 1909, Galerie Legrip held an “Exhibition of Modern Paintings”, where seven Rouen artists and one Parisian exhibited 24 canvases, including 5 canvases which gave an excellent insight into the works of Robert Pinchon. This exhibition was announced by a poster of C. la Broue, and P. Hans notes in the Normandy Worker “...We recognize the three inseparables Pinchon, Dumont and Tirvert going into raptures in front of a famous cathedral in flamboyant colors...” and he also wrote: “...A still life of Mr. Pinchon absorbs light to a great extent, the objects that he gathers together are more mundane and much more ‘stuff’ than those of Mr. Dumont...Also, Mr. Pinchon varies his technique in almost every work, proof of competency and confidence...”.

Then the artist prepared for his first Paris exhibition, thanks to the generous support of Francois Depeaux. This took place from March 15 to 25 at the Gallery of Modern Artists, at the experts Chaine and Simonson. At the age of 23, Pinchon faced the Paris populace with success, then exhibited at the Salon of Rouen Artists and, then, at the 38th Municipal Exhibition of Beaux Arts of Rouen. He also lent all his support to Pierre Dumont to set up the Normandy Society of Modern Painting and exhibited at Legrip.

In November, at the inauguration of the Depeaux Room at the Rouen Museum, he was particularly well represented with 4 canvases of the 52 paintings generously offered by the collector. The day of the opening, Pinchon rubbed elbows with such renowned painters as Lebourg, Raffaelli, Guillaumin, and with art critics Arsène Alexandre, Rovger Milès and his friends the Rouen painters. At the end of the year he was absent from the first exhibition of the Normandy Society of Modern Painting.

In 1910, he exhibited in May at Legrip, then at Havre and made the front page of the Rouen Gazette on September 10, with two articles: ‘The Man’ by A. Dupuys and ‘His Work’ by E. Morel - the former decorated with a caricature by C. La Broue, the latter by a self portrait. The following year started with a solo exhibition at Legrip, then Pinchon participated in the second Normandy Exhibition of Modern Painting where Pierre Dumont hung the portrait of his friend Pinchon, who sent three works of “Factories at Eauplet”. Side by side with Tirvert and Dumont, we find Robert Pinchon in Paris at the Gallery of Ancient and Contemporary Art with two canvases from November 20 through December 16.

In March 1912, he returned to the Exhibition of Rouen Artists with four canvases, then to the 7th Exhibition of the Society of Modern Painting from June 15 until July 15, which was held in the great hall of skating of the Lacroix Island in Rouen. The catalogue was prefaced by Elie Faure, and Guillaume Apollinaire came and spoke on the “Sublime Modern”. The 12 canvases sent by the artist were very important. Seduced by fauvism, this ardent colorist gave the fullness of his methods. The year 1913 saw three directions: The Legrip Salon, the Salon of Rouen Artists with 8 canvases, of which the “Rue de l’Epicerie” belonged to Mr. Bories, the patron of Narcisse Guilbert and the 4th exhibition of the Society of Modern Painting. In the catalogue of this exhibition, we find a caricature of C. LaBroue representing Pinchon, who exhibited 6 works, of which G. Dubosc writes “...A varied ensemble, he has never displayed himself as such a harmonious colorist as in this series of Pont Corneille and Flowers in the open air...”

After the last exhibition of the Society of Modern Painting where Pinchon hung 3 canvases, followed by the Salon of Rouen Artists with 6 canvases, real fire works took place. The opening took place on June 6, the same month, and he continued into July with an exhibition in Swansea, Wales, organized by Fr. Depeaux, called “L’Ecole de Rouen”. G. Dubosc was in charge of the catalogue and three canvases of Pinchon, owned by Fr. Depeaux, called “Wild Flowers at Water’s Edge” were exhibited among 43 paintings. On August 3, 1914, Germany declared war on France and the artist was mobilized on August 5th. He was sent to the front and was wounded in the leg at the battle of the Marne on September 16 in Berry-au-Bac. He convalesced in St-Céré, in the Lot, were he received a long letter from Dubosc dated October 18 “...The clan of painters has, of course, been dispersed...I met Dumont, who is fine, and who was preparing to go up to Paris, a little disconsolate, because ‘modern painting’ was being sold everywhere to the Germans...Tirvert had kept his oriental calm and serenity. I don’t know where Paul Pitt is, but La Brou was in the Engineers in Arras...Couchaux was also wounded...”.

Pinchon went back to the front as a machine gunner, but was wounded again. In May 1915, the Salon of Active Duty Soldiers took place, and the State acquired the “Quarry of Amfreville-la-Mivoie”. At the end of the year, Pierre Dumont organized the exhibition “For our soldiers, for our artists”. Three paintings of Pinchon were hung in this generous exhibition. The artist returned to the front and during the battle of Verdun, was captured in Thiaumont, and he was detailed to a rural farming community in Germany.

In 1917, he was represented in the 9th Salon of Rouen Artists, where an important retrospective, consecrated to Zacharie took place. The year 1918 passed in captivity, where, thanks to paints, pastels and oils, Pinchon could keep on producing. At the end of the year he escaped, crossed Switzerland, Italy and France and reached Rouen at the end of December. There, the exhibition of Painters of the Sea took place, organized by the French Naval League, at the Rouen Museum. Pinchon was represented here with four paintings.

Pinchon arrived at the family home, 49 rue Armand Carrel, which he had left four and a half years previously. He participated in the 10th Salon of Rouen Artists, where he exhibited the pastel; drawings of his convalescence in St. Céré, and of his captivity in Germany. During the summer he went to Honfleur and Lisieux, and, in September he vacationed in Brittany at Saint-Jean-du-Doigt and the surrounding area with Jean Charles Contel. He took part in the Autumn Salon in Paris with two other Rouen artists: Paul Mascart and Narcisse Guilbert. From January 26 to February 5, 1920, he exhibited at Galerie Legrip his series of canvases brought back from Brittany, and then some works at Havre in the lobby of the “La Cloche” newspaper, along with 70 paintings of Rouen artists. On October 11, Francois Depeaux, an exceptional collector and man of art, died. Right at the end of the year, on December 6 and 7, the collection of H. Perrot was sold at auction in Rouen - along with 20 Couchaux, 28 Dumont, 42 Guilbert and 39 Pinchon, among which was “Factory at Eauplet”.

On January 24, 1921, Robert A. Pinchon married Lise Bance and had three children. The artist lent his participation to the opening of the Modern Gallery at Rouen, but suffered from the loss of his friend Paul Pitt on February 7. He exhibited again at the Galerie Legrip, and his canvas “The Maraicher Garden” was bought from him at the 39th Municipal Exhibition of Beaux-Arts of Rouen for 800F.

The first part of the fabulous collection of Fr. Depeaux was dispersed on June 25 in Paris, and then on July 19 in Havre, and finally in Rouen on December 19. During these three vacations, forty canvases of Pinchon were sold.

During 1922 to 1923, Robert A. Pinchon rejoined the Salon of Rouen Artists, the Galerie Legrip, and also assisted the Modern Gallery in rue St. Romain.

In 1924, the artist developed an important activity: in February, at Legrip, then at the Salon of Rouen Artists, and finally at the Modern Gallery, with “Through Old Rouen” courtyards and streets, 38 large cartoons showing the most picturesque aspects of the town of 100 bells. The next year Pinchon took part in the Salon of Normandy Artists in Paris, exhibited at Legrip, and suffered the loss of his father. He then chose to move to Bois-Guillaume and to prepare for the collection.

It took place in Paris, at the Reitlinger Gallery, rue de la Boétie from February 16 to March 4, 1926. G. Dubosc, who had faithfully supported the artist, related in the Rouen newspaper of February 18, that this Parisian exhibition was going to give new momentum to the career of the painter. He again exhibited at the Galerie Legrip and at the Museum for the Salon of Rouen Artists. He did the same in 1927, where the year ended with a very important exhibition at the Reitlinger Gallery with 45 canvasses, from November 12 to 18. During the exhibition one of his canvases was purchased by the Ministry of Public Instruction and Beaux-Arts.

In 1928, he took part in two traditional Rouen exhibitions (Legrip and the Salon of Rouen Artists) but also joined two group exhibitions in Budapest and Varsovia, again exhibited at the Reitlinger Gallery, and at the end of the year in Brussels at the Kodak Gallery of Art. In the Rouen Gazette of December 19, 1936, replying to Gontran Sedille, while suffering a great economic crisis, R. Pinchon said: ...Have we or have we not returned in reality to the conditions before 1914? I remember this time and my debut. My friends and I held our first exhibition...naturally we didn’t sell anything! Happily we had our parents...but we needed pocket money, and our canvases ended up - for 20 francs - at father Perreau (sic)”.

After his exhibition at Reitlinger in January 1929, he took part in the Salon of the Society of Rouen Artists in Paris and became a regular member, then at the Salon of French Artists and, then, his annual exhibition at Legrip.

From 1930 to 1939, Robert Pinchon held regularly his three exhibitions, and exhibited twice again at the Salon of French Artists in 1930 and 1932; he became president of the Society of Rouen Artists in 1935, three years after having been named member of the Academy of Rouen.

The artist remained faithful to Rouen, its banks of the Seine, its old streets, but also the surrounding areas: Honfleur, Etretat, Fécamp, Dieppe. However, he also went to the Midi: Marseilles, St. Tropez, Gassing in 1934, and went twice to Brett in Brittany. In 1935, the Rouen editor, H. Defontaine published the text of Lucia Delarue-Mardrus “Rouen” and Pinchon did the illustrations: the following year, with the same editor, P. Choral gave “Normandy Cathedrals and Churches” from November 20 to 30. In the catalogue of this latter exhibition, where there were 41 paintings, are quoted press extracts: Chi. Kunstler of the artistic Figure, J. Normal of the Journal des Arts, and F. Guy of the Rouen newspaper: “There is no more need to praise the artist, everyone knows with what acuity he perceives and translates the effects of our Normandy atmosphere, of which his canvases remain as a living reflection”.

The second volume of “Rouen” was published in 1937. The same year, the artist illustrated “The Castles of Normandy” on a text of Jean de La Varende; he also bought a thatched cottage in Varende, and took part in the exhibitions of the House of Culture in 1937 and 1938.

The war surprised Pinchon and his family in the house in Varengeville on September 3, 1939. He stopped his Paris exhibitions, and his last exhibition took place at the Hotel de la Couronne, place du Vieux Marche in Rouen, in May and June 1941 under the patronage of Pierre Varenne, a man of letters.

After having lost two companions dear to his heart in 1942, Narcisse Guilbert on February 23 and Pierre Hode on December 15, Robert Antoine Pinchon died on January 9, 1943.

Work and Personality

P.R. Wolf perfectly described the man in the preface of the catalogue of April 1958 at the time of the exhibition of R.A. Pinchon and M. Louvrier at the Museum of Beaux-Arts of Rouen: “...In spite of his broad shoulders and rolling gait of a sailor, Pinchon was a timid man, more ‘listener’ than ‘speaker’, and who whinnied in a silent laugh, showing all his horsy teeth. When he composes, Pinchon never forgets the theme, and when he is in the presence of the theme he still composes. He has his natural arabesque which ‘sweeps’ the landscape sometimes towards wallcoverings, sometimes towards decor. We are just a little bit removed from an easy effect, but all is saved by the elegance and the distribution of a harmony, by a broad vision, by the pictorial quality, by the tenderness with which he caresses a tree, a cloud. Even when under the influence of Pierre Dumont, who throws into the Rouen School the leaven of his nervous anxiety of builder, Pinchon ‘beams’ as tender fauvist. He sometimes repeats himself from one theme to another, form one hour to the next on the same theme, but in the manner of a musician, more like a composer of songs than of symphonies, who tries his skill at variations...”

R.A. Pinchon’s beginnings have been influenced by the three Rouen masters: Lebourg, Delattre, Frechon. Some works, kept by members of the family easily show the sure influence of these three masters, but this period was very quickly exceeded by the astonishing vocation of colorist, which is that of the artist alone. This was brought to light under the stimulus of his friend Pierre Dumont and the Paris exhibitions, where Pinchon gave free rein to the magic of colors. From the war to the period 1925-1930, Pinchon became more and more the colorist, while ornamenting his art with a manifest decorative concern. His career ended with very designed landscapes where he attained perfect synthesis of the elements and made of himself the painter of Normandy light in the lineage of Lebourg.

His work comprises about 2,800 to 3,000 oils, some watercolors and gouaches, pastels and drawings, which are impossible to count.

Translated from the book L’Ecole de Rouen written by François Lespinasse
